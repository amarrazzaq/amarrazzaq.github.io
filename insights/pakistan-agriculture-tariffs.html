Can Pakistan's Agriculture Absorb the 29% Tariff from the Trump Administration?

Dr. Amar Razzaq

Associate Professor of Agricultural Economics

The recent announcement by the Trump administration to impose a 29% tariff on Pakistani agricultural imports has sent shockwaves through Pakistan's agricultural sector. As one of Pakistan's key export destinations, changes in U.S. trade policy can have significant implications for the country's economic stability and rural livelihoods. This article explores the potential impacts of these tariffs and examines whether Pakistan's agricultural sector has the resilience and adaptability to absorb such a substantial trade barrier.

Cotton harvest in Punjab, Pakistan. The textile sector represents a significant portion of Pakistani agricultural exports potentially affected by tariffs. Photo: Agricultural Department, Punjab

The Scale of the Challenge

Pakistan's agricultural exports to the United States currently total approximately $1.2 billion annually, representing about 8% of Pakistan's total agricultural exports. The 29% tariff would effectively increase prices for U.S. consumers by nearly one-third, potentially reducing demand for Pakistani products substantially. The sectors most vulnerable to these tariffs include textiles (particularly cotton-based products), rice, and mangoesâ€”all staples of Pakistan's agricultural export portfolio.

Initial economic modeling suggests that without adaptation measures, Pakistan could see a reduction of 35-40% in agricultural exports to the U.S. market. This translates to potential losses of $420-480 million annually and could affect the livelihoods of approximately 2.3 million farmers and agricultural workers directly or indirectly involved in export-oriented production.

Adaptive Capacity of Pakistan's Agricultural Sector

Pakistan's agricultural sector has demonstrated resilience in the face of previous economic shocks. Several factors will determine its capacity to adapt to these new tariffs:

Market Diversification: Pakistan has been actively pursuing agricultural export diversification, with growing markets in China, the Middle East, and Southeast Asia. The country's "Look East" policy has already reduced dependency on Western markets over the past decade, potentially cushioning some of the impact.

Value Addition: There is substantial room for increasing value addition in agricultural exports, which could help offset tariff impacts. Moving up the value chain from raw cotton to finished garments, for instance, could maintain export values even with reduced volumes.

Production Efficiency: The tariffs might accelerate much-needed efficiency improvements in production systems. My research on water markets and agricultural productivity suggests that farmers can reduce production costs by 15-20% through better resource allocation, potentially preserving competitiveness despite tariffs.

Policy Implications and Response Options

For Pakistani policymakers, several response options merit consideration:

Negotiated Exemptions: Diplomatic efforts could focus on securing exemptions for specific agricultural products, particularly those with humanitarian implications or mutual benefits.

Export Subsidies: Temporary export subsidies could help maintain market share while adaptation occurs, though these would need to be carefully designed to comply with WTO obligations.

Investment in Market Access: Accelerated efforts to meet phytosanitary and quality standards for alternative markets could open new opportunities for redirecting exports.

Research and Extension: Targeted agricultural research and extension services could help farmers transition to crops and varieties with stronger demand in alternative markets.

Case Study: Rice Exports

Pakistan's rice exports to the U.S. provide an illustrative case study. Premium basmati varieties have developed a loyal consumer base in the U.S., with relatively inelastic demand. Preliminary surveys of U.S. importers suggest they may absorb up to half of the tariff increase rather than lose market share, effectively reducing the impact on Pakistani exporters.

Additionally, Pakistan's rice sector has successfully penetrated new markets in Africa and Central Asia in recent years. The export infrastructure and relationships established during this expansion could facilitate redirecting supplies from the U.S. market if necessary.

Conclusion

While the 29% tariff presents a significant challenge, Pakistan's agricultural sector has multiple pathways to adaptation. The country's experience with previous trade disruptions, ongoing market diversification efforts, and potential for production efficiencies suggest the sector can weather this storm, albeit with short-term pain for certain subsectors and regions.

The most effective response will likely involve a combination of short-term measures to maintain market presence and longer-term structural adjustments in production and export patterns. For policymakers, the challenge will be balancing immediate support for affected communities with investments in longer-term resilience and competitiveness.

As Pakistan navigates this challenge, the situation underscores the importance of agricultural policy that builds adaptive capacity and reduces vulnerability to external shocks. In the long term, this may prove to be a catalyst for necessary reforms in Pakistan's agricultural sector.
